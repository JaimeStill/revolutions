# Marcus Webb

Marcus Webb is the first person who ever saw the same layer of the world at the same time Drew did. They met in Mr. Faulk's seventh-period computer applications class at Jasper Middle School in the fall of 2004, though they shared Mr. Chen's science class too --- Marcus was the kid two rows over with wire-rimmed glasses and handwriting so cramped it looked like code. The friendship caught on a Tuesday in October when Mr. Faulk showed an Excel formula and Marcus walked up afterward and said, "You noticed it too, right? It's not about adding numbers. It's about telling the machine what to do."

Marcus came from Rice Elementary, not Mendenhall. His father, David Webb, is an electrical engineer at Texas Instruments in Richardson, the kind of man who explains impedance at the dinner table the way other fathers explain football plays. His mother, Janet, teaches third grade in Richardson ISD. The Webb house is a one-story ranch in an older subdivision, smaller than the Mitchell place but full of half-assembled electronics and books about circuits. Marcus had already disassembled the family's old Compaq Presario before he met Drew --- motherboard on a towel on the garage floor, each component labeled with masking tape.

Where Drew knows systems and software architecture, Marcus knows hardware and networking. They are complementary without being competitive, which is the rarest configuration in a friendship between two boys who are both used to being the smartest person in the room. Their progression over three years tells the story: ugly HTML in sixth grade, dual-booting Ubuntu and running Apache in seventh, a functioning message board for Tran's gym by the summer before eighth. The message board was the first real thing --- real users, real database, real input validation problems when a gym member's kid broke the forum with angle brackets. Each failure taught them something with the same honest quality Drew loves about martial arts: the system does not care about your intentions.

Marcus has an easy social warmth that Drew observes and respects but does not try to replicate. Marcus can walk into a room and talk to anyone, not because he's performing confidence but because he genuinely likes people in a less analytical way than Drew does. Drew brought Marcus to Nana Carol's house, and Carol approved, asking Marcus about his soldering projects with the same seriousness she gives Drew's reading. Marcus brought Drew into the Webb household, where taking things apart was not tolerated but assumed.

They argue about software architecture the way other boys argue about sports teams. They can say "that's wrong" and mean it technically, not personally. Being wrong costs nothing with Marcus, which means being right means something. They have been fully honest with each other about ideas and thinking for three years. Emotional honesty entered the channel in September, when Drew told Marcus about Sofia on day two, and Marcus received it with two words: "I know."

In mid-October, the channel turned again. Drew came to Marcus between classes --- not to report but to ask for help. The Karen problem, the concert logistics, the question of how to introduce Sofia into his family's awareness. Marcus listened and cut to the structural answer the way he debugs a network issue: find the bottleneck, route around it. "So tell your dad first." Four words that rearranged Drew's understanding of his own family architecture.

This was the first time Marcus had operated as an emotional advisor --- not just receiving Drew's disclosures but actively solving for the relational problem. He picked the gray collared shirt for the concert. He understood that surface presentation was not performance but respect. The channel between them now runs across both registers in both directions: intellectual and emotional, giving and receiving. The well that once held only thinking has caught enough rain to be something else. Not a lake yet. But more than a well.
