# Sofia Reyes

Sofia Reyes is the first person Drew Mitchell ever reached for. Before the letter she was a presence he tracked through rooms --- the girl in honors English who said things that made him think for days, whose silence was not shyness but selectiveness, whose laugh in the hallway once produced something adjacent to jealousy he could only name in retrospect. After the letter she became a person, which is a different and more complicated thing than a presence.

She has been in the Jasper honors track since sixth grade, having come from a different elementary school. She plays violin in the school orchestra --- second chair, though this is a choice about temperament rather than ability, a distinction she seems to understand better than most adults do. In classrooms she speaks the way she does everything: precisely, after consideration, each word examined before it leaves. In Mrs. Kelley's English class the previous spring, during a unit on unreliable narrators, she said, "Every unreliable narrator thinks they're the reliable one. That's the whole point." Drew thought about it for two days. He is not someone who thinks about things other people say for two days.

She has dark hair she wore longer at the start of eighth grade than in seventh. She wore a green jacket on the first day of school. She opens her notebook before class starts and holds her pen the way a musician holds an instrument before the downbeat. Her handwriting is small, neat, angled slightly left.

What became clear on the second day of eighth grade is that Sofia does not open channels by accident. She read Drew's letter twice before she responded, which tells you how she processes things --- thoroughly, on her own schedule, without external pressure accelerating her timeline. When she approached him after English class on Tuesday, she did not perform warmth or enthusiasm. She told him what she'd noticed in the letter: that he had described her habit of thinking before speaking as "the point" rather than a problem. This mattered to her. She put the letter back in her bag. She kept it.

The courtyard lunch was twenty minutes of conversation that moved the way good conversation moves --- without visible effort, covering ground neither person had planned to cover. She told Drew about her summer in San Antonio, her grandmother's house, the sound of Spanish as an ambient language rather than a private one. When Drew explained his three-range martial arts framework, she reframed it as "learning three languages for the same conversation," which was not a correction but an expansion --- her mind working on the same material from a different direction, the way a second instrument enters a piece of music.

She said "same time tomorrow" when the bell rang, and the phrase was an offer, not a question. She sets the pace. She chose the courtyard, chose the time, chose to approach Drew rather than wait. She is deliberate about this the way she is deliberate about everything, and the deliberateness is not caution --- it is care. She does not give what she does not mean.

Sofia is now a gatekeeper in Drew's life, though not the way Drew's other gatekeepers operate. Karen gates through logistics and monitoring. Greg gates through trust earned by competence. Sofia gates through authenticity. The condition for continued access is that Drew remains the person who wrote the letter --- not a performance of that person, not a managed version, but the one who said what he noticed and meant it. She reads people carefully. She will know the difference.
