# Sofia Reyes

Sofia Reyes is the girl Drew wrote a letter to on the last Sunday night before eighth grade and handed it to on the first Monday morning. Before that, she was something less defined and more persistent --- a presence he'd been tracking through rooms since seventh grade without fully admitting he was doing it.

She has been in the honors track at Jasper since sixth grade, having come from a different elementary school. She plays violin in the school orchestra. In classrooms, she is quiet in the way that certain people are quiet not because they have nothing to say but because they are unwilling to say things that don't meet their own standard. When she does speak, the room tends to pause. In Mrs. Kelley's English class the previous spring, during a discussion of unreliable narrators, she said, "Every unreliable narrator thinks they're the reliable one. That's the whole point." Drew went home and thought about it for two days. He is not someone who thinks about things other people say for two days.

She has dark hair that she wore longer at the start of eighth grade than she had in seventh. She wore a green jacket on the first day of school that shouldn't have worked but did. She opens her notebook before class starts and holds her pen the way a musician holds an instrument before the downbeat --- ready, waiting, prepared for something to begin. Her handwriting, visible from three seats away, is small and neat and angled slightly left.

Drew does not know her yet. That is the precise truth of it. He knows what she looks like when she's thinking. He knows the sound of her voice when she says something sharp enough to surprise a teacher. He knows that she once laughed at something Marcus said in the hallway, and that the laugh produced something in Drew that he could identify as adjacent to jealousy only in retrospect. He knows the specific sentence she said about unreliable narrators. He knows nothing about her family, her neighborhood, what she does on Saturdays, what music she listens to besides what she plays, what she thinks about when she's not in a classroom being observed by a boy she may not have noticed observing her.

What he handed her was not a confession but an opening --- a folded piece of notebook paper that said, in the precise and unhedged voice Drew uses when the filter steps aside, that he'd like to know her better. She took the letter, looked at it, looked at him, and said "Okay" in a tone that was neither warm nor cold but simply receiving. Then Drew walked away, and the next move became hers.

She is now a gatekeeper in Drew's life, though she did not ask for the role. Whether the channel he opened becomes bidirectional --- whether she reads the letter and feels something, or reads it and feels nothing, or doesn't read it at all --- will determine whether this thread of Drew's story leads somewhere new or folds back into the familiar architecture of wanting things and managing the wanting into silence. The honors track at Jasper is small. Third period is every day. Whatever she decides, she and Drew will be in the same room for it.
