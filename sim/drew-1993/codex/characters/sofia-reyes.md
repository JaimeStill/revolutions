# Sofia Reyes

Sofia Reyes is the first person Drew Mitchell ever reached for. Before the letter she was a presence he tracked through rooms --- the girl in honors English who said things that made him think for days, whose silence was not shyness but selectiveness, whose laugh in the hallway once produced something adjacent to jealousy he could only name in retrospect. After the letter she became a person, which is a different and more complicated thing than a presence.

She has been in the Jasper honors track since sixth grade, having come from a different elementary school. She plays violin in the school orchestra --- first violins, capable of carrying an eight-bar solo in the Barber Adagio without the section behind her. In classrooms she speaks the way she does everything: precisely, after consideration, each word examined before it leaves. In Mrs. Kelley's English class the previous spring, during a unit on unreliable narrators, she said, "Every unreliable narrator thinks they're the reliable one. That's the whole point." Drew thought about it for two days. He is not someone who thinks about things other people say for two days.

She has dark hair she wore longer at the start of eighth grade than in seventh. She wore a green jacket on the first day of school. She opens her notebook before class starts and holds her pen the way a musician holds an instrument before the downbeat. Her handwriting is small, neat, angled slightly left.

What became clear on the second day of eighth grade is that Sofia does not open channels by accident. She read Drew's letter twice before she responded, which tells you how she processes things --- thoroughly, on her own schedule, without external pressure accelerating her timeline. When she approached him after English class on Tuesday, she did not perform warmth or enthusiasm. She told him what she'd noticed in the letter: that he had described her habit of thinking before speaking as "the point" rather than a problem. This mattered to her. She put the letter back in her bag. She kept it.

The courtyard lunches ran for weeks --- 12:15, the far bench, brown paper bags, twenty minutes. She told Drew about her summer in San Antonio, her grandmother's house, the sound of Spanish as an ambient language rather than a private one. When Drew explained his three-range martial arts framework, she reframed it as "learning three languages for the same conversation," which was not a correction but an expansion --- her mind working on the same material from a different direction, the way a second instrument enters a piece of music.

In mid-October, Sofia invited Drew to her orchestra concert and to meet her parents. The invitation was two things at once: an offer and a test she did not call a test. She was bringing him into the part of her life the courtyard hadn't touched --- her family, her music, the world she goes home to. She trusted that the person who wrote the letter would still be present in the lobby under her mother's questions.

On stage, during the Barber, Sofia played an eight-bar solo --- exposed, no section to hide behind. The courtyard version of Sofia is deliberate and verbal, choosing her words the way a musician chooses notes. The stage version is the thing itself: discipline made audible, precision made physical, years of practice compressed into a passage where there is no place to hide and no need to. Three languages for the same conversation --- her metaphor, applied to Drew's martial arts, now applying to her. The courtyard, the stage, and the letter she kept in her bag are three languages saying the same thing.

After the concert, she crossed the lobby to Drew and introduced him to Elena and Luis. She stood slightly to the side and watched the exchange with the careful satisfaction of someone whose bet was paying off. She had known what her mother would do, what her father would ask. She had trusted that Drew would be himself. He was. The word "captivating" escaped him in Elena's presence, and Sofia heard it, and it was not retracted. Her parents invited Drew to dinner. She looked back once before leaving the lobby --- not a wave, not a smile, a look. A private signal between two people who now share something that has a shape.

Sofia is no longer only wading in. The gate she holds --- access to reciprocal connection, governed by authenticity rather than performance --- is more open than it was before the concert. She brought Drew into her family's world and he held up. What follows is whether he uses the opening, and she will watch for that the way she watches for everything: carefully, on her own schedule, without rushing the thing she is building.
