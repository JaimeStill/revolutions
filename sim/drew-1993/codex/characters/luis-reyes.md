# Luis Reyes

Luis Reyes is an engineer, and he communicates like one. Where Elena leads with questions, Luis leads with observation. He stood slightly behind his wife in the Jasper lobby, watching Drew talk to Elena the way Drew imagines Luis watches systems at work --- not for what they're doing, but for whether the structure underneath is sound. He is not a quiet man in the way that shyness produces quiet. He is quiet in the way that competence produces quiet: he has learned that the important information arrives when you stop talking.

He asked Drew one question. One. "The martial arts. Most kids try something and move on. You stay. What --- you get bored?"

The question had the shape of a challenge and the function of a probe. It was the same kind of question Mr. Tran asked when he wanted to separate the students who trained from the students who showed up. Luis was not interested in what Drew did. He was interested in why Drew stayed with what he did, and the distinction between those two questions is the distinction between polite curiosity and genuine assessment.

Drew told him that difficulty gives you a space to be uncomfortable in, a challenge to evolve through. That if it were easy, it wouldn't be worth committing to. Luis nodded --- not the social nod, not the parental nod. The real one, the one that means the structure held when you tested it. "That's right," he said, and the two words carried the weight of a man who had arrived at the same conclusion through his own years of staying with hard things and recognized it in someone else.

He shook Drew's hand before the family left. The handshake was firm and offered to a young man, not a child. Luis had not said much. He had said enough. Drew carries the nod the way he carries Mr. Tran's corrections --- as evidence that a man who understands commitment saw it in him and confirmed it without elaboration.

Luis and Greg Mitchell share a frequency, though they have never met. Both communicate through competence. Both measure people by what they do rather than what they say about doing it. Both offered Drew something in a brief exchange that a longer conversation might have diluted: the recognition of a man who has been where you are and sees that you're building something real.
