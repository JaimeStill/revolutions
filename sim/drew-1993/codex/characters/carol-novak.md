# Carol Novak

Carol Novak is Karen's mother and the most important intellectual relationship in Drew's life. She started picking him up from school in 2000, when Greg's restructuring and Karen's extra shifts left a gap in the afternoons, and what began as childcare became something richer.

Nana Carol's house operates on different rules. She gives Drew books --- not children's books, but whatever is on her shelves --- and lets him talk about them. She taught him to garden: to identify herbs by smell, to repot seedlings at the right moment, to read soil the way Greg reads a wiring diagram. He fixed her back porch railing with wood glue and a C-clamp, and she thanked him the way you thank a colleague, not a child. In her garage he built a working pulley from clothesline and a paint can, and when he explained the mechanics, she listened as if his explanation mattered.

The Saturday afternoons have never stopped. By fourteen, the books have changed --- Siddhartha, Zen and the Art of Motorcycle Maintenance, a dog-eared copy of Godel, Escher, Bach that Drew got sixty pages into and put down and picked up again three months later. Carol gives him books that are always slightly above his level and trusts him to grow into them. She is the only person who gets the unfiltered version of Drew's thinking --- not because he chooses to be honest with her, but because in her presence the filter simply does not engage. When Drew brought Marcus to her house, Carol approved quietly, the way she does everything, asking Marcus about his soldering projects with the same seriousness she gives Drew's reading. She recognized in Marcus what she had recognized in Drew: a mind that was working, not performing.

Carol is the one relationship in Drew's life that operates without transaction, without performance, without the careful management of what the other person can handle. She is giving him something no one else provides, and the thing she gives has not changed since he was eight: the experience of being seen for what he thinks, not what he does. What has changed is that Drew, at fourteen, is beginning to understand how rare that is.
