# Greg Mitchell

Greg Mitchell is an engineer at Alcatel, deep in the Plano Telecom Corridor --- a man who speaks in solutions rather than feelings. He survived the 2000 restructuring that took three men on his team, came home carrying a cardboard box of their desk photos, and went back to work the next Monday. He was promoted in 2003. He drives to Oklahoma for disaster relief and says things like "This changes everything" only when he means it.

His relationship with Drew was built on a currency of competence. Greg does not say he is proud of his son; he hands him a wrench. When Drew started mowing the lawn without being asked, Greg responded by trusting him with the sprinkler timer, then the oil check on the Camry, then the loose hinge on the garage door. Each task was a small transfer of authority, and Drew understood every one.

The martial arts opened a second channel. Greg attended one of Drew's tournaments --- sat in the bleachers without cheering loudly, arms folded across his chest, simply present. Afterward, in the truck, Drew gave a technical debrief: the scoring, the strategy, where he'd been outmatched. Greg asked questions with the attentiveness he gave engineering problems. It was the most sustained conversation they'd had in months, and it happened because Drew had given Greg something Greg knew how to respond to: a domain with rules, a performance that could be analyzed, a son who was competent in terms Greg could see and measure.

On a Thursday evening in October, Drew came to the garage and opened a third channel --- the one neither of them had known was there.

Greg was sorting hardware into coffee cans. Drew said he needed a minute. What Drew framed as logistics --- a concert, a ride, a Friday night --- was something deeper, and Greg heard it. He sat down on the rolling stool with the cracked vinyl, the one Karen has been threatening to throw out since summer. He did not stand over his son. He sat, and the sitting was itself a kind of attention.

He asked real questions. Not Karen's questions --- not the diagnostic ones angled toward risk --- but the questions of a man who was genuinely interested in the shape of his son's life. What was her name. What did she play. How long had the lunches been going. And then Greg offered the thing Drew had not known he needed: "You want me to tell your mom?" Not hiding it. Just timing. The understanding that introducing Sofia into Karen's awareness required someone who knew Karen's frequencies, and that Drew did not have to carry that particular weight alone.

Greg told Karen that evening. Whatever the conversation cost him --- the small sting Karen would have felt at being told second, the smoothing it required --- Drew heard none of it through the walls. Greg had covered the flank. Drew experienced, for the first time, his father as an active ally in the emotional landscape, not just a passive authority in the competence domain.

Then Greg asked about the tech stack. Not as deflection but as bridge. "No, tell me the actual thing. What's the stack?" He listened to Drew describe Apache and MySQL and the forum software modifications with the attention of a man at Alcatel who recognized, in his son's message board, something he understood from the inside. The conversation lasted twenty minutes. It was the longest they had talked about something that was not a task.

Greg is becoming a father in a new register. The competence channel is still there --- it will always be there, because it is how both of them are built. But the garage conversation revealed that the channel was wider than either of them had tested. Greg knows the window where Drew still comes to him is finite. He can feel it the way an engineer feels the tolerance narrowing on a part --- not with panic, but with the precise awareness of a man who understands that some opportunities do not recur. He is building a secondary bridge through the tech projects, the Alcatel interest, the questions about the stack. He is also, for the first time, standing in the space where his son's emotional life lives, and he is not leaving.
