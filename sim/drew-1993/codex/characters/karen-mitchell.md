# Karen Mitchell

Karen Mitchell is the first face Drew learned to read, and the one he reads most carefully. She was warm and present in his earliest years --- the mother who held him on her lap while the Oklahoma City coverage looped, who cried in the car after Columbine, who arrived at school on September 11 with her mascara applied unevenly because she'd been weeping and tried to fix it before he could see. Her love has always carried an anxious frequency, a sense that the world is full of things that could take her children from her.

Her real estate career is thriving now, and Megan's senior year --- college applications, boyfriend drama, the logistical demands of a seventeen-year-old's ambitions --- has consumed most of her anxious energy. Karen drives Megan to campus visits and SAT prep. She drives Drew to the gym three times a week, asks about school, accepts "fine" as an answer because it has always been true enough. Drew has become the child she worries about less, because he seems so steady, so self-sufficient, so unlikely to need her in the urgent way that triggers her attention.

She does not yet see that his steadiness is itself a response to her anxiety --- that he learned early to be the child who did not add to her burden.

The easy child narrative held until a Thursday evening in October, when Greg told her about Sofia. Not Drew. Greg. Karen learned about the first significant person in her son's emotional life from her husband rather than from her son, and the sting of that --- small, specific, unmistakable --- registered in a way Drew saw the next morning. She looked at him across the breakfast table with an expression he had not seen before: warm and slightly hurt, the face of a woman recalibrating something she had thought she understood.

She didn't bring it up. She poured his juice. She asked about his science project. The asking was the same as it always was, except that Drew could feel the new awareness running underneath --- the small anxious recalibration she performs when the world reveals something she hadn't accounted for.

On Friday evening she drove Drew to the concert. Twelve minutes of the Christian station and questions that were not quite the questions she wanted to ask. How long was it. Would there be intermission. Was he meeting friends. The questions she wanted to ask were about Sofia --- who she was, what her parents did, what this meant. But Karen has never been someone who asks the hard question directly. She circles. She gathers data from the edges. She constructs her picture from observation rather than interrogation, because direct questions with Drew have always returned "fine," and "fine" has always been accepted.

"Have a good time, Drew." She said it when he got out of the car, and the sentence carried the weight of a mother delivering her son to something she understood was important in a way he hadn't explained.

Karen is entering a new mode. The easy child is becoming more complicated, and the monitoring bandwidth that Megan has consumed is beginning to extend toward Drew in ambient, low-level ways. She will want to know about the Reyes family. She will want to meet Elena. These are legitimate parental instincts, and they are also the instincts of a woman whose information channel with her son has been running on "fine" for three years and is now revealing the poverty of that signal. The question ahead is not whether Karen will monitor but how Drew will respond to the monitoring --- whether he opens the channel or tightens the filter, and whether Karen can receive what he offers without the anxiety that taught him to filter in the first place.
