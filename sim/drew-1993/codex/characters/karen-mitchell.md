# Karen Mitchell

Karen Mitchell is the first face Drew learned to read, and the one he read most carefully. She was warm and present in his earliest years --- the mother who held him on her lap while the Oklahoma City coverage looped, who cried in the car after Columbine, who arrived at school on September 11 with her mascara applied unevenly because she'd been weeping and tried to fix it before he could see. Her love has always carried an anxious frequency, a sense that the world is full of things that could take her children from her.

Her real estate career is thriving now, and Megan's senior year --- college applications, boyfriend drama, the logistical demands of a seventeen-year-old's ambitions --- has consumed most of her anxious energy. Karen drives Megan to campus visits and SAT prep. She drives Drew to the gym three times a week, asks about school, accepts "fine" as an answer because it has always been true enough. Drew has become the child she worries about less, because he seems so steady, so self-sufficient, so unlikely to need her in the urgent way that triggers her attention.

She does not yet see that his steadiness is itself a response to her anxiety --- that he learned early to be the child who did not add to her burden. Three years of reliable behavior have made Karen broadly permissive with Drew. She trusts his judgment. The main constraint is logistics: she has to drive him places, and Megan's schedule competes for her bandwidth. Drew is starting to feel the edges of wanting more autonomy than the driving situation allows, but he does not push, because pushing is not something the easy child does. Karen is looking away from him at precisely the age when looking closely would matter most, and Drew is making it easy for her to look away.
