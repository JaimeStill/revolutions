# Chapter: Ranges (2004--2007)

The book was thin, red-covered, and wedged between a John le Carre novel and a gardening reference on Nana Carol's middle shelf. Drew pulled it out one Saturday in June and read the first page standing up. *The Art of War* was not what he expected --- not battle plans and troop movements, but something closer to a philosophy of preparation. He sat down on Carol's living room floor and read it cover to cover. Then he read it again.

What drew him was not the martial content. It was the systematization. Kyle Briggs had been a problem he'd survived, not a problem he'd solved. The encounters had ended well enough --- stand still, show nothing, wait it out --- but Drew could feel the gap between what had happened and what might have happened if Kyle had been less bored or more committed. He had no training, no framework, no plan beyond stillness. And stillness was a bet on the other person losing interest, which is a bet you lose exactly once.

He researched the way he researched everything: methodically, on the family computer in the study, toggling between browser tabs while the dial-up modem held the phone line hostage. He discovered a three-range framework for fighting --- muay thai for striking distance, wrestling for the clinch and takedowns, jiu-jitsu for the ground --- and the logic of it satisfied him the way structural logic always did. Three ranges. Three disciplines. Coverage without gaps. He printed articles from martial arts forums. He made a chart on notebook paper.

Drew presented his parents with a plan rather than a request. This was important: he had learned that Greg respected proposals and Karen resisted surprises. He explained what he wanted to study, why, what it would cost, and which gym he'd identified. Tran's Martial Arts, off Parker Road --- a strip-mall gym between a nail salon and a check-cashing place. No banners, no trophy cases in the lobby, no children's birthday party packages. Just mats, bags, and a back office where Mr. Tran did paperwork between classes.

Mr. Tran was a compact man in his fifties who had fought professionally in Thailand and spoke in corrections rather than praise. He shook Drew's hand on the first day and said, "You're small. That's fine. Small means fast if you work." The gym smelled of vinyl and sweat and the eucalyptus oil Mr. Tran used on sore muscles. The other students ranged from teenagers to middle-aged men. There were no other eleven-year-olds. Drew liked this.

The training was the first thing in Drew's life that he could not intellectualize past. He understood the mechanics of a roundhouse kick within twenty minutes. He could not throw one properly for three months. The gap between knowing and doing was physical, inarguable, and humbling in a way that school had never been. School-easy Drew --- the boy who absorbed curriculum like atmosphere --- did not exist here. Here, understanding earned you nothing. Only repetition earned you anything, and repetition meant getting hit, falling wrong, holding pads until your forearms bruised, and coming back Thursday to do it again. He loved it. Not the pain, but the honesty of it. The feedback was immediate, embodied, and could not be faked.

---

Jasper Middle School sat on a plot of flat land off Jupiter Road, bigger than Mendenhall in every dimension --- wider hallways, more students, a cafeteria that seated three hundred. Orientation night in August 2004 was a controlled flood of information: class lists taped to the gym wall, locker combinations on index cards, parents photographing schedules on their Razr phones. Drew worked the room like a checklist. Homeroom: Mrs. Halverson, Room 114. Science with Mr. Chen. Computer applications seventh period with Mr. Faulk. He memorized the route between classes and identified the bathrooms nearest each one. Karen hovered near the PTA table. Greg had not come.

Mrs. Halverson's homeroom became the anchor of Drew's sixth-grade mornings --- a quiet room where the fluorescent lights hummed and Mrs. Halverson called everyone "friend" and meant it in a way that made the word feel like an actual offer rather than a pleasantry. She was a short woman who wore reading glasses on a beaded chain and kept a jar of peppermints on her desk that she never explicitly offered but never moved out of reach. Drew liked her immediately, which meant he relaxed in her room by a degree that was visible only if you knew what his baseline tension looked like.

The first weeks were about establishing what Drew would later think of as a battle rhythm --- the phrase was Mr. Tran's, borrowed from his own training camps. Walk in. Scan the room. Find the structure. Operate within it. The academic work was not hard; Plano ISD's curriculum was rigorous, but Drew's study habits had been set since third grade. The harder problem was the cafeteria.

The cafeteria was a social map drawn in real time, six times a day, five days a week. By the second week, the tables had sorted themselves: athletes along the windows, skaters near the vending machines, band kids in the back corner, quiet kids scattered in the spaces between. Connor Huang migrated to the skater table within the first month, pulled there by gravity Drew could see but did not share. They still nodded at each other. The nods were friendly and final.

Drew did not pick a table. He drifted. He sat with the athletes one day and asked a kid named James about the football schedule, not because he cared about football but because he was curious about what organized James's week. He sat with the band kids and listened to an argument about whether trumpet or saxophone was harder. He sat with the quiet kids and found that some of them were quiet because they were shy and some were quiet because they were watching, the way he watched.

This was not a strategy, exactly. It was a genuine orientation --- Drew was interested in how groups worked, what the rules were inside each one, what made a person belong or not belong. He found that most groups were organized around a shared enthusiasm and a shared way of performing that enthusiasm, and that the differences between groups were less about substance than about dialect. The athletes talked about effort; the skaters talked about style; the band kids talked about precision. These were different words for the same thing: the feeling of getting better at something that mattered to you. Drew could speak all of these dialects because he was actually listening, and people can tell the difference between someone who is listening and someone who is performing interest.

By the end of sixth grade, Drew occupied an unusual social position: he belonged to no group and was welcome in most of them. He was the kid who could sit anywhere without it being weird, because he never sat anywhere as a claim. Some kids read this as aloofness. Most read it as what it was --- a boy who was curious about everybody and competitive with nobody. It was not a strategy for popularity. It was a genuine expression of how Drew's mind worked: differences were lenses, not walls. Each group showed him a different facet of how people organized themselves, and he found all of them interesting.

---

Marcus Webb first registered as a presence in Mr. Faulk's seventh-period computer applications class. He was a Rice Elementary kid --- Drew didn't know him from Mendenhall --- with wire-rimmed glasses and a habit of chewing on the end of his pen when he was thinking. They also shared Mr. Chen's science class, where Marcus sat two rows over and took notes in a handwriting so cramped it looked like code.

The friendship caught on a Tuesday in October, the way friendships catch at that age --- not through any dramatic event but through a moment of recognition. Mr. Faulk had demonstrated an Excel formula, something about using VLOOKUP to pull data from one spreadsheet into another. Drew understood it immediately, but what he understood was not the formula. It was the principle underneath: you were giving the machine a set of instructions, a logic chain, and the machine executed it without variance. The spreadsheet was an interface, not a tool. The tool was the logic.

After class, Marcus walked up. "You noticed it too, right?" he said. "It's not about adding numbers. It's about telling the machine what to do."

Drew had never had someone see the same layer simultaneously. Connor had been a friend of proximity and parallel play. Marcus was a friend of recognition. The difference was seismic.

They started spending Wednesday afternoons together, first at Marcus's house on Roundrock Trail, later alternating with Drew's place and Nana Carol's. Marcus's father, David Webb, was an electrical engineer at Texas Instruments in Richardson --- a tall, patient man who kept oscilloscopes in the garage and explained impedance at the dinner table the way other fathers explained football plays. His mother, Janet, taught third grade in Richardson ISD and had the organized warmth of someone whose professional life was built around making children feel safe. Their house was smaller than the Mitchell place, a one-story ranch in an older subdivision, but it was full of half-assembled electronics and books about circuits, and it felt like a house where taking things apart to understand them was not just tolerated but assumed.

Marcus had already taken apart the family's old Compaq Presario by the time Drew met him --- had the motherboard on a towel on the garage floor, each component labeled with masking tape in that cramped handwriting. Drew's knowledge was different: he understood the internet as an architecture, had been reading about TCP/IP and DNS from articles he found following links from Nana Carol's bookshelf to web searches. Marcus knew hardware. Drew knew systems. They fit.

Their first project was a website --- if you could call it that. Two pages of HTML so ugly it would have been rejected by a GeoCities template. But the ugliness was instructive: they learned that structure and presentation were separate concerns, which led them to CSS, which led to the first real conversation about design as a discipline distinct from engineering. Drew remembers the afternoon they got a stylesheet to work --- Marcus's bedroom, a CRT monitor casting blue light on their faces, a Coke can on the desk leaving a ring on a printout of W3C documentation. They had made something that looked intentional rather than accidental, and the satisfaction was disproportionate to the accomplishment. They were twelve.

Seventh grade brought Ubuntu. Marcus had read about Linux on a hardware forum and burned a live CD from an ISO downloaded over the family's DSL connection. They dual-booted Marcus's machine on a Saturday in January, taking turns typing commands into a terminal, consulting a printout of instructions from the Ubuntu wiki. When the desktop loaded --- the brown and orange of Dapper Drake, garish and triumphant --- they sat back and looked at it as if they'd wired their own electricity. Which, in a sense, they had: they were now operating outside the consumer layer, in the space where software was built rather than merely used.

Then Apache. Getting a web server running on Marcus's Ubuntu machine, serving a page over the local network, watching Drew's laptop display a page that was being generated by Marcus's desktop three feet away --- this was the alchemy moment. The gap between "the internet" as an abstraction and "a computer serving files to another computer" as a physical reality collapsed, and Drew understood, in his body and not just his mind, that the entire web was just machines talking to machines through protocols that humans had written. Every website he had ever visited was someone's Apache or IIS instance responding to his browser's request. The magic was not magic. It was architecture, all the way down.

That summer --- the summer before eighth grade --- they built something real. Mr. Tran had mentioned, in his offhand way, that the gym's schedule was a mess, that parents kept calling to ask about class times, that he wished there were a better way. Drew and Marcus built a message board. PHP, MySQL, hosted on a shared server Marcus's dad helped them rent for twelve dollars a month. It was simple --- a schedule page, a forum for members, an announcements section Mr. Tran could update. But it had real users. Real people typed things into forms that Drew had built, and those inputs were stored in a database Marcus had designed, and the pages rendered dynamically, and nothing caught fire.

They learned about input validation when a gym member's kid posted a string of angle brackets that broke the forum layout. They learned about database normalization when the schedule table became unwieldy. They learned about backups when they didn't have one and the hosting company had a blip. Each failure was a lesson with the same honest feedback quality that Drew loved about martial arts: the system did not care about your intentions. It cared about your code.

Marcus became Drew's best friend not through any declaration but through accumulation --- Wednesday afternoons, Saturday projects, a running AIM conversation that never quite ended, just paused overnight. Drew brought Marcus to Nana Carol's house, and Carol approved in her quiet way, asking Marcus about his soldering projects and listening to his answers with the same seriousness she gave Drew's. Marcus brought Drew into the Webb household, where David explained transistors and Janet made sandwiches and nobody asked Drew to perform a social role he hadn't chosen.

They were fully honest with each other about ideas. They argued about software architecture the way other boys argued about sports teams --- with conviction, without stakes. They could say "that's wrong" and mean it technically, not personally. Drew had never had that before. Being wrong cost nothing with Marcus, which meant being right meant something.

What they were not honest about was feeling. Neither boy had the vocabulary or the inclination. Drew's emotional inhibition found a comfortable echo in Marcus's temperament --- Marcus was warm and socially easy in a way Drew was not, but his emotional life was similarly channeled into building and solving rather than discussing. Their friendship was deep but it was deep in a specific direction, like a well rather than a lake. This did not feel like a limitation at fourteen. It felt like enough.

---

At the gym, Drew's body was becoming something he had built rather than something he merely inhabited. Three years of training had accumulated in ways that surprised him when he caught himself in a mirror --- not bigger, exactly, but organized. Lean. The kind of build that came from throwing kicks and sprawling on mats rather than from lifting weights. He moved differently than he had at eleven, more deliberately, with an economy that came from knowing where his limbs were and what they could do. His peers noticed, though Drew did not perform it. The physical presence was simply there, the way a callus is there --- the residue of repetition.

His first tournament was in the spring of 2005 --- a small muay thai event in a hotel ballroom in Mesquite, fluorescent lights and folding chairs and the smell of Tiger Balm. Three bouts. He won the first on points, lost the second to a boy who was faster and had been training longer, won the third against an opponent who telegraphed everything. Third place. The trophy was cheap brass on a fake marble base, and it didn't matter. What mattered was the data: where his guard dropped under pressure, which combinations he defaulted to when tired, how fear felt in his body and what it did to his technique. He drove home with Greg --- Greg had insisted on coming, had sat in the bleachers without cheering loudly, had simply been there --- and Drew gave a technical debrief that Greg followed with the attentiveness he gave engineering problems.

In seventh grade, Drew joined the Jasper wrestling team. Coach Davis ran practice like a drill sergeant --- whistle, sprint, whistle, shoot, whistle, sprawl. But Drew had three years of jiu-jitsu ground work and muay thai hip movement that the other seventh-graders did not, and the advantage was immediate. He was not the strongest kid on the team. He was the kid who already understood leverage, who could feel a takedown developing before it arrived, whose hips moved instinctively in directions that Coach Davis had to teach everyone else by drill. Coach Davis noticed within two weeks and began using Drew for demonstrations, which was its own currency in the wrestling room's hierarchy.

Two more tournaments in seventh grade. He won one --- a dual meet against a Richardson school --- and placed second at a regional, losing in the final to a kid with twenty pounds on him who simply sat on Drew's legs and waited out the clock. The loss taught him something the wins did not: that preparation has limits, that some advantages cannot be overcome with technique, that the honest thing was to study the gap rather than resent it.

Mr. Tran, watching all of this, began to let Drew help teach the younger kids' fundamentals class. It started informally --- "Show him the stance, Drew" --- and became a Tuesday and Thursday routine. Drew discovered something unexpected: teaching forced a deeper understanding than performing. You could throw a proper roundhouse kick on muscle memory alone, but explaining the hip rotation to a nine-year-old who kept squaring up required you to decompose the movement into principles you'd never consciously articulated. It was the same thing he'd found with computer science: the gap between using a tool and understanding a system. Mr. Tran, who communicated primarily through correction and the occasional nod of approval, offered life advice disguised as fighting advice: "You don't control the other person. You control the space. The space does the rest."

---

At home, the household had shifted around Drew without anyone acknowledging it. Megan was seventeen now, a senior at Plano Senior High, and college applications consumed the family's anxious energy the way job security had consumed it in 2001. Karen drove Megan to campus visits and SAT prep and volleyball practice, and the logistics of Megan's ambitions colonized the family calendar. Drew had more unsupervised time than ever. He used it for training, for projects with Marcus, for reading at Carol's. Karen still drove him to the gym three times a week, and she asked about school, and he said "fine," and it was true enough. He was the easy child --- the one who did not slam doors, did not cry about boyfriends, did not need to be monitored. Karen accepted this as a gift rather than recognizing it as a performance, and Drew did not blame her for it, because he did not yet recognize it as a performance either.

Greg attended one of Drew's tournaments and sat in the bleachers with his arms folded across his chest. He did not cheer. He watched. Afterward, in the truck, Drew explained what had happened technically --- the scoring, the strategy, where he'd been outmatched. Greg asked questions. It was the most sustained conversation they'd had in months, and it happened because Drew had given Greg something Greg knew how to respond to: a domain with rules, a performance that could be analyzed, a son who was competent in terms Greg could see and measure. The martial arts had become a direct channel between them, one that did not require either of them to be vulnerable.

Megan's emotional life, meanwhile, was a weather system Drew observed from across the house. Boyfriend drama arrived with the regularity of seasons --- the fights, the tearful phone calls, the slammed bedroom door, the reconciliation, the next crisis. Drew watched this the way he watched everything: systematically, looking for patterns. He was not conscious of what he was doing. He was building a dataset about what happens when emotions are not managed, when the inhibition that came so naturally to him was absent. It frightened him slightly, not Megan herself but the possibility that this kind of volatility existed inside everyone and that his own composure was a choice rather than a nature. He did not pursue this thought. He filed it.

Nana Carol remained the constant. Saturday afternoons at her house had not changed since he was eight, except that now Marcus sometimes came along, and Carol treated Marcus the way she treated Drew --- as someone whose mind was worth engaging. She asked Marcus about his soldering projects. She asked Drew about his reading. She gave Drew books that were always slightly above his level and trusted him to grow into them: Siddhartha, Zen and the Art of Motorcycle Maintenance, a dog-eared copy of Godel, Escher, Bach that Drew got sixty pages into and put down and picked up again three months later. Carol was the only person who got the unfiltered version of Drew's thinking --- not because he chose to be honest with her, but because in her presence the filter simply did not engage. She was the one relationship in his life that operated without transaction, without performance, without the careful management of what the other person could handle.

---

It is August 2007. Drew is fourteen, about to start eighth grade at Jasper Middle School. He is five-foot-eight, lean, and carries himself with a physical economy that reads as older than his age. He has a best friend who sees the same layer of the world he does. He has a martial arts practice that has taught him the relationship between preparation and honest feedback. He has a grandmother who treats his mind as something worth feeding. He has a social position that depends on being between groups rather than inside one, and he has not yet been tested on whether that position holds when the spaces between groups become contested ground. Kyle Briggs is still in the ecosystem, bigger now, part of the athlete power structure that organizes eighth-grade hierarchy. Connor Huang is a friendly stranger. Megan is leaving for college. The household is about to contract, and Drew is about to become the only child in a house where the easy child has always been the invisible one.

He does not know it yet, but the next year will ask him a question he has never been asked: not whether he can figure things out, but whether he can want things for himself --- not because they are useful, not because they earn trust, but because they matter. He has spent three years building competence across three domains, and the competence is real. The question is whether competence is enough to navigate a world that is about to demand something else entirely.
