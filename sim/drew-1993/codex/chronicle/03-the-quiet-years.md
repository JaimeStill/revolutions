# Interlude: The Quiet Years (2002--2004)

The years between eight and eleven were not dramatic. They were the years in which Drew's patterns set like concrete. At home, he mowed the lawn and reset the sprinkler timer without being asked. Greg noticed, and began trusting Drew with small tasks --- tightening a loose hinge, checking the oil level in the Camry. Greg showed pride through delegation, not words, and Drew learned to read that currency fluently.

At Nana Carol's, the world was different. She gave him books and let him talk. He learned to identify herbs by smell in her garden, repotted her tomato seedlings in spring, and fixed the back porch railing with wood glue and a C-clamp. Carol was his most important intellectual relationship --- the only adult who treated his mind as something worth feeding rather than managing.

At school, he was near the top of his class without performing it. He didn't raise his hand to show he knew the answer; he raised it when no one else would. A boy named Kyle Briggs, the fifth-grade alpha, tested him twice --- once with words, once with a shove. Drew didn't flinch either time. He didn't fight back, either. He just stood there and waited, and Kyle, finding no purchase, moved on to softer targets.

Connor Huang was still nominally his best friend, but the gap was widening. Connor had discovered skateboarding and video games and, more critically, had begun to care about social optics --- who was watching, who approved. Drew had not arrived at that awareness yet, or perhaps had decided it wasn't worth the energy. Either way, they were drifting, and neither boy had the vocabulary to name it.

---

It is May 2004. Drew is eleven years old, kneeling in Nana Carol's backyard with dirt under his fingernails, repotting tomato seedlings into larger containers. Fifth grade is ending. Summer stretches ahead, and beyond it, Jasper Middle School --- a new building full of new hierarchies he has not yet learned to navigate. He is competent, steady, quietly watchful. He measures his worth by his usefulness and has not yet been forced to ask whether that metric is sufficient. The world has taught him that safety is earned, not given. He has not yet learned what happens when competence isn't enough.
