# Columbine

On April 20, 1999, two students at Columbine High School in Littleton, Colorado walked into their school carrying firearms and homemade explosives. Over the course of forty-nine minutes, they killed twelve students and one teacher, wounded twenty-four others, and then killed themselves in the school library. It was, at that time, the deadliest school shooting in American history.

The shooters were not outsiders. They were enrolled students — seniors, weeks from graduation. They had planned the attack for over a year, assembling weapons and explosives while attending classes, eating in the cafeteria, and interacting with the people they intended to kill. This fact — that the violence came from inside the school, from students known to their victims — was what made Columbine psychologically distinct from prior acts of violence. It was not an intrusion. It was a betrayal of the basic social contract that makes schools function: the assumption that the people sitting next to you are not planning to murder you.

The event transformed American public education. Within months, schools across the country installed metal detectors, hired security officers, and implemented "lockdown drills" — a protocol in which students practice hiding silently in darkened classrooms while an authority figure checks the doors. These drills became routine within two years. An entire generation of American children grew up practicing, regularly and without adequate explanation, how to behave if someone came to their school to kill them.

For parents, Columbine added school — the one institution they were required by law to send their children to — to the list of places that might not be safe. In communities like Plano, which were already processing the heroin epidemic's assault on suburban safety, Columbine compounded the fear. The parenting culture tightened further. Schools became sites of both education and security theater. The relationship between children and the institutions meant to serve them acquired a new, unspoken layer of anxiety.

Drew was six. He did not understand what had happened at Columbine. He understood that his teachers were behaving wrong — the pitch of their voices, the way they gathered in doorways and spoke in low tones. He understood that Karen told him school was safe and that her voice did not match her words. He asked her, "How do you know?" and she did not have a good answer. The lockdown drills began shortly after. The children practiced being very quiet in dark rooms, and nobody explained why in terms that made sense, because the truth did not make sense to adults either.
