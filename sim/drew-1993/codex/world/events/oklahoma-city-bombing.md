# Oklahoma City Bombing

On April 19, 1995, a man named Timothy McVeigh parked a rented truck packed with nearly five thousand pounds of ammonium nitrate fertilizer and fuel oil outside the Alfred P. Murrah Federal Building in Oklahoma City, Oklahoma. The explosion killed 168 people, including 19 children in a daycare center on the building's second floor. It was the deadliest act of domestic terrorism in United States history.

McVeigh was a Gulf War veteran who had become radicalized by anti-government militia ideology. He chose the federal building deliberately — it housed offices of the Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco, and Firearms, the agency he blamed for the government's violent siege of a religious compound in Waco, Texas, exactly two years earlier. The bombing was, in his mind, retaliation.

The attack shattered a particular American assumption: that mass violence came from outside, from foreign actors with foreign grievances. Oklahoma City proved that the threat could come from within — from a white, military-decorated citizen of the heartland. This was psychologically destabilizing in a way that took years to fully register. The vocabulary for "domestic terrorism" barely existed in mainstream American discourse before this event.

For families in the southern plains states — Texas, Oklahoma, Kansas — the bombing was geographically and culturally close. Oklahoma City was not a faraway metropolis; it was the place you drove to for a weekend, where your cousin worked, where your church had a sister congregation. Greg Mitchell drove there for disaster relief and was gone for four days. The television footage — the sheared face of the building, the firefighter carrying a dead child — played on loop for weeks.

The bombing's lasting effect on parenting culture was subtle but real. It added a new category of fear: that ordinary buildings in ordinary cities could be destroyed without warning. For parents of young children in the mid-1990s, it was the first crack in the assumption that the institutions surrounding their families — schools, offices, government buildings — were inherently safe spaces.

Drew was two years old. He understood nothing about the event. What he understood was that his father was gone and his mother was holding him differently.
