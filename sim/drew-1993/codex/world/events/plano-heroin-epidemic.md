# Plano Heroin Epidemic

In the late 1990s, the city of Plano, Texas — one of the wealthiest and most meticulously planned suburbs in the United States — experienced a cluster of heroin overdose deaths among its teenagers. Between 1996 and 1999, more than a dozen young people died of heroin overdoses in a community that had been explicitly designed to keep its children safe.

The deaths were shocking not because heroin was new to American suburbs, but because Plano was supposed to be the antidote to urban danger. Its parents had moved there precisely to escape the risks of the city — crime, poverty, drugs, bad schools. They had bought houses in subdivisions with names like Willow Bend and Chase Oaks, enrolled their children in highly rated schools, and built lives around the premise that the right zip code could protect a family. The heroin deaths proved that premise hollow.

The victims were not the children that the community's mental model of "drug users" predicted. They were athletes, honor students, kids from two-parent households with college funds. The drug entered through social networks — passed at parties, introduced by older friends, available cheaply because Mexican black tar heroin had flooded the Dallas-Fort Worth corridor. A dose cost less than a case of beer.

Plano's institutional response was surveillance. The school district introduced drug-sniffing dogs. Parents tightened curfews, monitored phone calls, searched bedrooms. The police department created a narcotics task force specifically targeting the suburb. The community's approach to child-rearing shifted from trust-based to monitoring-based — not overnight, but steadily, over the course of two or three years.

For younger children like Drew, who was four when the deaths peaked, the epidemic was invisible as an event but visible as a change in atmosphere. Doors locked earlier. Parents asked more questions. The ambient assumption that Plano was safe — that living there meant your children were safe — acquired a hairline fracture that would widen with each subsequent crisis.
