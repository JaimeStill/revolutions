# Alcatel and the Telecom Corridor

The Telecom Corridor is an informal name for a concentration of telecommunications and technology companies along the Dallas North Tollway and George Bush Turnpike in Plano and Richardson, Texas. At its peak in the late 1990s, the corridor housed the North American headquarters or major offices of Alcatel, Ericsson, Fujitsu, Nortel Networks, EDS (Electronic Data Systems), Texas Instruments, and dozens of smaller firms. It was one of the densest concentrations of telecom engineering talent in the world.

The corridor's growth was driven by the telecommunications boom of the 1990s — the buildout of fiber optic networks, the expansion of mobile phone infrastructure, and the speculative investment that accompanied the rise of the commercial internet. Companies recruited engineers from across the country with competitive salaries and the promise of suburban quality of life. The surrounding neighborhoods filled with families who had moved to Plano specifically because one parent had a job in a glass building off the turnpike.

Alcatel was a French telecommunications equipment manufacturer that maintained a substantial campus in Plano. Greg Mitchell works there as an engineer, managing a team that builds network infrastructure. His career at Alcatel represents the typical Telecom Corridor trajectory: steady employment through the boom years, the shock of the 2000 restructuring when the dot-com bubble burst, survival through the layoffs, and a promotion in 2003 as the industry stabilized at a smaller scale.

The corridor's economic cycle — boom, bust, partial recovery — maps directly onto the Mitchell family's financial and emotional history. During the boom years, Greg's salary supported a comfortable suburban life. During the bust, the possibility of job loss introduced a new anxiety into the household. After the restructuring, stability returned but with a permanent awareness of its fragility. Greg now manages a team in a company that has already demonstrated its willingness to shed people. The cardboard box he carried home in 2000 — containing the desk photos of three colleagues who no longer worked there — sits in the Mitchell family's emotional memory whether anyone mentions it or not.

For Drew, Alcatel is the place his father goes when he leaves in the morning and the reason he comes home tired. It is a glass building he has driven past but never entered. It is the source of the family's stability and, after the restructuring, the source of a quiet fear he absorbed without anyone naming it.
