# Jasper Middle School

Jasper Middle School sits on a plot of flat land off Jupiter Road in Plano, Texas, serving grades six through eight for the Plano Independent School District. It is bigger than Mendenhall Elementary in every dimension --- wider hallways, a cafeteria that seats three hundred, a gym with bleachers, a weight room the football coaches guard like territory. Drew attended from 2004 to 2008.

The academic rigor is inherited from the district: Plano ISD is one of the highest-performing districts in Texas, and the expectations are baked into the curriculum without anyone needing to announce them. Teachers assign more homework than most suburban districts. The gifted and talented track sorts students early and visibly. Standardized test preparation is woven into the school year the way weather is woven into agriculture --- not a separate event but a background condition that shapes everything else.

But the real institution at Jasper is the cafeteria. The cafeteria is where the social map is drawn in real time, five days a week, and the map hardens as the years progress. Sixth grade is fluid: kids sit where there is space, try different tables, migrate. By seventh grade, the tables have sorted --- athletes along the windows, skaters near the vending machines, band kids in the back corner, quiet kids in the spaces between. By eighth grade, the sorting is complete and the spaces between groups become contested ground. Social neutrality, which was tolerated in sixth grade, begins to carry a cost.

The school's institutional culture rewards the same things Plano's broader culture rewards: academic achievement, athletic participation, politeness, compliance. A student who performs well on tests and does not cause trouble is a student the system considers successful. The wrestling program, run by Coach Davis, is competitive within the district. The computer applications class, taught by Mr. Faulk, is where Drew and Marcus Webb first recognized each other --- a seventh-period elective that most students treated as an easy credit and two students treated as a portal.
