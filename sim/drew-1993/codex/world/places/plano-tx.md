# Plano, Texas

Plano in the late 1990s and early 2000s is a suburb that has mistaken order for safety. The lawns are manicured. The subdivisions have names like Willow Bend and Chase Oaks. The streets are wide, clean, and navigable by minivan. It is the kind of place people move to because nothing is supposed to happen there, and then things happen anyway.

The Telecom Corridor runs through it like a vein of money and anxiety. Alcatel, EDS, Ericsson, Fujitsu, Nortel --- the names line the access roads off the George Bush Turnpike, glass buildings full of engineers like Greg Mitchell who build infrastructure for a future that keeps arriving in unexpected shapes. The dot-com boom filled these offices. The bust emptied some of them. Restructurings ripple through neighborhoods --- one month a man is grilling in his driveway, the next month his family is gone and a realtor's sign stands in the St. Augustine grass.

The megachurches anchor the community like aircraft carriers. First United Methodist, Prestonwood Baptist, Christ the King --- enormous campuses with gymnasiums and coffee bars and preschool programs. They provide the social architecture that the subdivisions, with their privacy fences and three-car garages, do not. Karen sends the children to First United Methodist preschool not because she is particularly devout but because that is where children go.

Beneath the order runs a persistent low hum of fear. The Plano heroin epidemic of 1997 killed teenagers --- not the teenagers you expected, but the ones from good families, the ones with college plans. The community's response was surveillance: tighter curfews, more watchful parents, drug-sniffing dogs at the high school. Then Columbine in 1999 added a new fear to the rotation --- not drugs but violence, not from outside but from within, from children themselves. Then September 11 layered national dread over local anxiety, and the lockdown drills that had started after Columbine acquired a new gravity.

The result is a place that looks like safety and feels like vigilance. Children grow up in houses with central air and two living rooms, riding their bikes on sidewalks that lead to cul-de-sacs, attending schools with good test scores and locked doors. The parents work hard, worry constantly, and communicate their worry through control rather than conversation. It is a good place to raise a child, everyone agrees. It is also a place where a quiet, watchful boy can learn that the world is dangerous without anyone ever sitting him down and saying so --- because the danger is encoded in the architecture itself, in the locked doors and the hushed voices and the television that never quite turns off.
