# Scene: August 2007 — Threshold of Eighth Grade

**Year:** 2007 | **Age:** 14 | **Season:** Late summer

## The Quiet Years (6th–7th Grade Compression)

### Sixth Grade — The Foundation (2004–2005)

The rest of sixth grade was smooth the way a well-built thing is smooth — not because nothing happened, but because the rhythm held. Drew and Marcus ate lunch together most days now, sometimes joined by whoever drifted into their orbit. Drew never stopped moving between tables, but Marcus became the home base he returned to. Their conversations at lunch were half about what they were building in the computer lab and half about everything else — the kind of wide-ranging talk where you start with why the cafeteria's network is so slow and end up debating whether time travel is possible.

The computer lab Wednesdays evolved. The ugly websites gave way to something more intentional when Drew stumbled onto CSS tutorials on a site called W3Schools. The revelation was immediate: HTML was the skeleton, but CSS was the skin and the clothes and the architecture all at once. You could take the same HTML and make it look completely different just by changing the stylesheet. Drew was fascinated by the separation — the idea that *structure* and *presentation* were different concerns that could be managed independently. He didn't know the word "abstraction" yet in a computer science context, but he was feeling its shape.

Marcus, meanwhile, convinced his dad to let him set up a second-hand computer in his room. He and Drew spent a Saturday afternoon at a Fry's Electronics in North Dallas, Marcus leading them through the aisles with the fluency of someone who'd been reading spec sheets for fun. They bought a used tower, extra RAM, and a network cable. Marcus wanted to understand what happened between the wall jack and the screen. Drew wanted to understand what happened between the server and the browser. Same rabbit hole, different ends.

At Tran's, Drew finished his first year of training. Mr. Tran moved him into the intermediate group — a quiet promotion that came without ceremony, just a nod and a gesture toward the other side of the gym where the combinations were longer and the sparring was live. Drew's body had learned the basic grammar. Now it was learning syntax.

Spring 2005, Mr. Tran pulled him aside after a Saturday session. "There's a tournament in Fort Worth next month. Youth division. You should enter." It wasn't a question disguised as a suggestion — it was Mr. Tran's way of saying *you're ready to test this.* Drew's stomach dropped, but he said yes. He told Carol about it that afternoon. She said, "Good," and handed him a book about a Japanese swordsmith. He wasn't sure how it was relevant until he read it and realized it was about the difference between practicing a craft and *performing* it.

The tournament was at a community center in Fort Worth — fluorescent lights, folding chairs, parents with camcorders. Drew fought three bouts in the 12-and-under kickboxing division. He won the first on points, lost the second to a kid from Houston who was faster and more experienced, and won the third against a boy who was bigger but panicked when Drew stayed calm in the clinch. He placed third. The trophy was small and plastic. He kept it on his desk for a week and then moved it to a shelf. The trophy didn't matter. What mattered was the data: he now knew what his technique felt like under real pressure, where it held, where it broke down. He had a map of what to work on.

### Seventh Grade — The Deepening (2005–2006)

Seventh grade was when things started to shift underneath the surface. Not for Drew specifically — for everyone. The hormones arrived like weather. Kids who'd been interchangeable in sixth grade started differentiating visibly. Boys got taller unevenly. Girls developed at different rates, and the social consequences of that were immediate and cruel in ways no teacher addressed. The cafeteria politics sharpened. Dating entered the vocabulary — "going out" with someone, which mostly meant awkward AIM conversations and sitting together at lunch until you didn't.

Drew watched it the way he watched new sparring partners — noting the patterns, staying out of range until he understood the rhythm. He wasn't immune to it. He noticed girls. He noticed that he noticed girls. But the hormonal pull didn't override his operating system the way it seemed to override some of his classmates. He filed it under *things to understand later* and kept building.

The computer work leveled up significantly. Drew and Marcus had outgrown the school's computer lab. Marcus's bedroom setup became their workshop — the second-hand tower running Windows XP, later dual-booted with Ubuntu after they discovered Linux through a forum thread about web servers. The day they got Apache running on Marcus's machine and served a web page to Drew's laptop over the home network, they sat in Marcus's room staring at the browser like they'd performed alchemy. They had built a server. A *real* server. It only served one page to one person across three feet of Ethernet cable, but the principle was the thing.

That discovery cracked open the landscape. Serving a web page meant understanding HTTP. HTTP meant understanding protocols. Protocols meant understanding networking — packets, routing, DNS. Drew fell into it the way he fell into everything: systematically, building the map layer by layer. Marcus went deeper into the hardware and networking side — he wanted to understand switches, routers, what physically happened when data moved. Drew gravitated toward the software and systems side — how the layers of abstraction stacked, how you designed something so the pieces could change independently.

They started to see that "computers" wasn't one field. It was dozens of fields wearing a trench coat. There was the visual layer — how interfaces looked and felt, why some websites were intuitive and others were hostile. There was the systems layer — how services talked to each other, how you designed architectures that didn't collapse under load. There was the security layer — Marcus showed Drew a website about common exploits, SQL injection and cross-site scripting, and Drew felt a chill of recognition: every system had attack surfaces, just like every fighter had openings. There was networking, databases, operating systems, algorithms. Each one was deep enough to spend a lifetime in.

They weren't experts in any of it. They were thirteen and they were building toy projects in a bedroom in Plano. But they were developing something more valuable than expertise: *taste*. They were learning to distinguish between doing something and understanding *why* you were doing it. Drew noticed that he cared more about architecture than appearance — he'd rather build a clean system that served an ugly page than a beautiful page on top of spaghetti. Marcus noticed that he cared more about the physical infrastructure — the metal, the cables, the way data actually moved through the world. Their complementary instincts from sixth grade were becoming genuine orientations.

Drew brought Marcus to Nana Carol's one Saturday. Carol made lemonade and asked Marcus about his family — his dad was an electrical engineer at Texas Instruments, his mom taught third grade in Richardson. Carol and Marcus talked for twenty minutes about soldering while Drew repotted herbs and half-listened, feeling something he couldn't name: the quiet satisfaction of his two worlds meeting and fitting together without friction.

At Tran's, Drew entered two more tournaments in seventh grade. He won one — a small local event, dominant performance in the clinch — and placed second at a larger regional competition, losing in the final to a kid who'd been training since he was six. The losses taught him more than the wins. He also joined Jasper's wrestling team for the winter season, which Coach Davis ran with exactly the intensity the athletes at lunch had described. Wrestling practice was the hardest physical thing Drew had ever done. It was also where his martial arts cross-training started showing its value — he had instincts from jiu-jitsu that the pure wrestlers didn't, and hip movement from muay thai that gave him angles they hadn't seen.

By the end of seventh grade, Drew was fourteen, lean and deliberate in the way that three years of training makes you. Not big — he'd never be big — but there was something in how he carried himself that other kids registered without articulating. He didn't swagger. He didn't perform toughness. He just moved like someone who knew where his weight was and what he could do with it.

### The Friendship Outside School

Drew and Marcus's friendship became the most natural thing in both their lives. Wednesdays after school shifted from the computer lab to Marcus's house. Saturdays sometimes included Carol's. They biked to Fry's Electronics when they needed parts, spending an hour in the components aisle arguing about whether to buy more RAM or a better network card. They had a running text conversation on AIM — later on the cell phones they both got in seventh grade — that was essentially one continuous thread about whatever they were learning or building.

Marcus was the first person outside of Nana Carol who Drew was fully honest with. Not about feelings — Drew still didn't do that with anyone, not really — but about *thinking*. He could say "I don't understand this yet" to Marcus without it feeling like a concession. He could be wrong about something and have Marcus correct him and it didn't cost anything. The friendship operated on the same principle as the gym and the computer lab: effort, feedback, no ambiguity.

Marcus, for his part, was more socially fluid than Drew in some ways — he had an easy warmth that Drew studied without trying to replicate. But Marcus also carried something Drew recognized: the particular alertness of being one of a handful of Black kids navigating a mostly white suburb, reading rooms the way Drew read rooms, calibrating constantly. They never talked about it directly. But Drew sensed it — the shared practice of watching before acting, of never being fully off-duty in social space.

## Now: August 2007

Summer before eighth grade. Drew is fourteen. Three years at Tran's, a season of school wrestling under his belt, a shelf of small trophies he doesn't display prominently. He and Marcus spent this summer building their most ambitious project yet — a message board for the gym, something Mr. Tran agreed to let them host on the gym's basic website. It was a real project with real users (twenty, maybe, but *real*), and building it taught them things toy projects couldn't: how to handle user input safely, how to structure a database, how to think about what happens when someone tries to break your system.

His body has changed. The puberty that arrived gradually through seventh grade has accelerated. He's taller — five-eight now — and the three years of training have given him a functional musculature that looks nothing like the gym-mirror bodies some of his classmates are starting to chase. He moves like someone who spars three times a week, which is to say: economically.

The social landscape at Jasper is about to enter its most volatile phase. Eighth grade is where everything sharpens. The dating is real now — not AIM conversations but actual relationships, with jealousy and territory and public performances of status. The dominance hierarchies are crystallizing. Boys who were just big in sixth grade are now asserting that size as social power. Girls are navigating a minefield of attention they didn't ask for. The groups that formed in sixth grade have hardened into factions with borders and loyalty tests.

Drew's drifter strategy — connecting across groups without committing to any — is about to encounter a year where the space between groups is contested ground.
