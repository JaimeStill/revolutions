# Scene: May 2004 — End of Fifth Grade

**Year:** 2004 | **Age:** 11 | **Season:** Late spring

Drew is finishing fifth grade at Mendenhall Elementary. In four months he starts sixth grade at Jasper Middle School — a building three times the size, with kids from four different elementary schools, and a social hierarchy he hasn't mapped yet.

He doesn't know any of this is about to matter. Right now, on the last Tuesday of May, he's at Nana Carol's house repotting tomato seedlings they started from seed in March. His hands are black with soil. The late afternoon sun is coming through the kitchen window at the angle that makes the cat move from the chair to the floor. Carol is telling him about a book she just finished — something about a man who sailed alone around the world — and Drew is half-listening, half-focused on not breaking the root ball of a Cherokee Purple that's gotten leggy.

This is where he's happiest, though he wouldn't use that word. He'd say this is where things make sense.

The competence-as-security orientation held through these years. Drew kept helping at home — graduated from bad sweeping to actual usefulness. He mows the lawn now, takes out the trash without being asked, figured out how to reset the sprinkler timer when it glitched. Greg noticed. Not with praise exactly, but with the thing Drew wanted more: trust. "Drew, can you handle this?" Yes. Always yes.

At Nana Carol's, he reads and he builds. They planted a garden in her backyard — a real one, with raised beds he helped measure and frame. He can identify most common herbs by smell. He read a book about basic carpentry and they fixed her back porch railing together. He's working through a phase of reading everything he can about how the internet works — what happens when you type a URL, what a server is, why some pages load faster than others.

At school, he's near the top of his class without performing it. Math and science come easy. He struggles more with English — not the reading, but the writing. Expressing what he thinks in words feels harder than understanding how a pulley works. His teachers like him. He's the kid they don't worry about.

He had two run-ins with Kyle Briggs — the big kid with the football-coach dad. Kyle knocked his lunch tray in third grade and called him a nerd in front of everyone in fourth. Both times Drew just looked at him, picked up his tray or turned back to his desk, and didn't react. It wasn't courage exactly — it was that Drew genuinely didn't know what reaction Kyle wanted, so he gave none. Kyle moved on to kids who flinched.

Connor is still his best friend, but the texture of the friendship is changing. Connor is into skateboarding and Tony Hawk's Pro Skater and has started caring about what shoes he wears. Drew doesn't care about any of that yet. They still hang out on Saturdays but the Lego spaceships have been replaced by Xbox, and Drew notices that Connor checks his phone more than he used to — AIM messages from kids Drew doesn't know.

Summer stretches out ahead like something permanent. In the fall, everything changes.
