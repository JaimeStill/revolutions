# Scene: November 12, 1993 — Plano Presbyterian Hospital

The fluorescent light is the first thing, though he won't remember it. A cold white hum above a warm body — Karen Mitchell, thirty-one, sweat-damp hair pushed back from her forehead, holding a boy she's waited eight months to name. Drew. Andrew James Mitchell, seven pounds eleven ounces, born at 4:47 AM on a Friday, which means Greg almost missed it. He'd been on a late call with the Alcatel team in Richardson when Karen's water broke, and Nana Carol — who'd been staying in the guest room for two weeks, just in case — drove her to the hospital in the Camry while Greg chased them across 75 in his Accord.

He made it. He's here now, standing at the edge of the bed with his tie loosened, holding a Styrofoam cup of coffee he keeps forgetting to drink. Megan is at home with the neighbor, Mrs. Park, probably awake and furious about it.

The room smells like antiseptic and something warmer underneath. Karen traces a finger along the baby's ear — a small, perfect ear, folded like a shell — and says something so quiet that Greg has to lean in. "He's got your hands," she says, though the hands are curled into fists the size of walnuts and don't look like anyone's yet.

The window faces east. In two hours the November sun will come up over the flat grid of Collin County — over the half-built subdivisions and the retention ponds and the churches and the strip malls and the headquarters of companies that sell things no one can see or touch. The sky will be enormous and pale. The live oaks along Jupiter Road will hold their leaves a little longer.

For now, the room is small and warm and complete.

---

Three days later, Karen carries Drew through the front door of 4812 Willow Bend Court, a four-bedroom in the Willowcreek subdivision. The house is two years old. The nursery is painted sage green. There is a mobile above the crib — little wooden stars that Nana Carol found at a craft fair in Denton — and a rocking chair that Greg's mother shipped from Michigan.

Megan watches from the hallway. She is three. She has been told she is a big sister now, and that this is exciting. She does not look excited. She looks like someone studying a problem.

Karen sits in the rocking chair. Drew roots. Latches. The house settles around them — the hum of central air, the tick of the kitchen clock, Greg talking to someone on the cordless phone in the garage. Outside, a landscaping crew blows leaves at the house across the street.

This is the world. This house. This mother. This steady mechanical breath of suburban comfort. Everything Drew will become begins with what happens in the space between Karen's heartbeat and his own.

She is tired. She has been tired for weeks. But she holds him like she is building something.
