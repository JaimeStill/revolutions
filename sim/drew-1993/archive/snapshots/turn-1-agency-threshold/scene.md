# Scene: October 2001 — Mendenhall Elementary, Plano, TX

**Year:** 2001 | **Age:** 8 | **Season:** Early fall

Five weeks after the towers. The flags are everywhere — on lawns, on cars, on the marquee outside Tom Thumb. Mrs. Pollard's class made paper flags and taped them to the windows. The principal started doing the Pledge of Allegiance over the intercom every morning, which they already did, but now he does it slower.

Drew doesn't know how to say what's different. Everything looks the same — the same brick school, the same four-square court with the crack down the middle, the same cafeteria that smells like floor wax and rectangle pizza. But the adults are different. They're trying too hard. Mrs. Pollard smiles more than she used to and it doesn't reach all the way.

At home, Greg watches the news every night now. He used to watch SportsCenter. Karen bought a flag for the front porch and another one for the Camry antenna. They go to church on Sunday and also on Wednesday night now, which they didn't before. Pastor Dave talks about evil and Drew doesn't know if he means the men on the planes or something bigger.

The thing Drew can't stop thinking about is this: on the morning it happened, before anyone told him what was going on, he knew. Not the details — not planes, not towers — but the *feel* of it. The way Mrs. Pollard's hand shook when she picked up the phone. The way the intercom clicked on and the principal's voice was wrong. Drew knew the world had broken before anyone explained how.

He's been sitting with that. The knowing-before-knowing. It makes him feel strange — like he has a radio inside him tuned to a frequency nobody told him about.

---

It's a Tuesday in October. Nana Carol picks him up because Karen is showing houses. They go to Nana Carol's place — the small brick house on Custer Road with the overstuffed bookshelves and the cat named Joni Mitchell. Nana Carol makes grilled cheese and doesn't turn on the TV.

Drew is doing his math worksheet at the kitchen table when he says, without looking up: "Nana, are more bad things going to happen?"

Carol sets down her coffee. She doesn't answer right away, which is one of the things Drew likes about her — she doesn't fill silence with the first thing that comes to mind.

"Yes," she says. "Some will."

Drew looks up.

"But most days are just days," she says. "Most days, you make your grilled cheese and you do your math and the cat sits in the sun and nothing happens at all. The bad things are real, but they're not most things."

Drew thinks about this. Then he says: "Dad says everything changed."

Carol is quiet for a moment. "Your dad is scared," she says. "Scared people see the world differently for a while."

This is the first time an adult has told Drew that another adult is scared. He doesn't know what to do with it yet. But he keeps it.
